About

Hi! I'm Beatriz :)
I’m a philosophy grad student based in Lisbon, working at the intersection of philosophy of mathematics, cognition, and AI. My research focuses mainly on the limits of computation (human or artificial) and on how formal theories of logic and mathematics relate to real cognition.

Since publishing my first paper at 21 (literature is my second passion!) and later joining the same journal as a reviewer, I became especially invested in the art of clear argumentation, careful writing, and collaborative academic work. These experiences deeply influenced my current projects and my big‑time goal of contributing to a more methodologically self‑aware cognitive science.

On the Research page you'll find current projects and developing ideas that bridge technical work and conceptual analysis. I also included a Reading Recommendations page, where I collect papers and books that have been important for my thinking and that I often suggest to friends, students, and colleagues. If any of this overlaps with your interests—or if you'd like to discuss collaborations, RA work, or supervision—feel free to get in touch with me via the Contact page.

Research

Specializing in epistemology, philosophy of mind, and cognitive foundations of mathematics.

Computation Scientific Realism Phenomenology Strong AI
  • Mathematical Cognition and innateness debates
  • Philosophy of Science in AI development

My research explores the limits of computation in human and artificial minds, drawing on philosophy of mathematics, logic, and cognitive science. I'm especially interested in how formal systems model (and sometimes fail to model) abstraction, representation, and reasoning, and in what these limitations imply for ambitious claims about AI and cognitive architectures.

Recommendations

Some books I've been reading or recommending for people who want to think about logic, math, and minds.

Absolute Provability and Safe Knowledge of Axioms — Timothy Williamson

A great paper. A gateway drug to deeper Theoretical Mathematics debates.

An Introduction to Gödel's Theorems — Peter Smith

The clearest guide I know to Gödel's incompleteness theorems.

Psychosemantics - Jerry Fodor

An excellent launchpad to the incredible world of CCTM and Computation Theories.

Brain Bisection and The Unity of Consciousness - Thomas Nagel

Split Brain cases give us great insight into the deeper functioning of the human brain.
